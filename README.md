# Netflix_Analysis

# An Analysis of Netflix Data From Kaggle Using Pandas and Business 
I analyzed the full Netflix Movies & TV Shows dataset from Kaggle to understand how Netflix built and evolved its global content library — from its early U.S. catalog to its international expansion and the rise of Netflix Originals. By cleaning the data, categorizing ratings, and visualizing trends over time, a clear strategic story emerged.

# Key Findings
1. Netflix’s early catalog strategy relied heavily on older, likely inexpensive films.
Across 2008–2012, the average age of movies added was often 10–20+ years old, reflecting a cost‑efficient approach to building a library quickly. This allowed Netflix to enter streaming with breadth before securing rights to newer titles.
2. TV shows initially lagged behind movies but became strategically central after 2016
TV additions were minimal in the early years, but the average age of TV content dropped sharply after 2016, coinciding with the rise of Netflix Originals, which are typically added the same year they’re produced.
3. A major inflection point occurred in 2015–2016.
We see a dramatic surge in both movies and TV shows added during this period. This aligns with:
    - Netflix’s global expansion into 130+ countries
    - The acceleration of original content production
    - A shift away from reliance on U.S. studio licensing
4. Movies and TV shows diverged in age trends after 2016.
    - TV shows got younger every year due to Originals.
    - Movies got older, reflecting:
        - shrinking access to new studio films
        - increased licensing of international back catalogs
        - slower development cycles for original films
5. Netflix’s international expansion reshaped its content mix.
    - A massive surge in Indian content after 2016
        - India was the breakout market due to its huge population and robust domestic film industry.
    - Strong growth from the UK
    - A complete absence of China due to regulatory barriers
6. Netflix’s catalog skews toward mature audiences.
When ratings are grouped into children / young adult / adult categories, the majority of titles fall into adult or young adult segments. Netflix’s content strategy is clearly oriented toward mature viewers rather than children’s programming.
7. Content growth correlates with major movements in Netflix’s stock price.
Overlaying stock price with content additions shows:
    - A strong upward trend in stock price during the 2015–2019 content boom
    - A plateau and decline as content growth slowed in 2020–2021
    - A clear relationship between catalog expansion, international rollout, and market confidence
This suggests that investors responded strongly to Netflix’s rapid scaling of its global library.
